FIVE SECRETS TO A GREAT WORKSHOP BY EMMA K. SMITH-STEVENS, TEACHING ASSISTANT AT UF

1. Be engaged. Participate! The greater number of writers sharing insights, the more productive, interesting and multilayered workshop becomes.


2. Be specific. Frame your comments and suggestions in a way that will be useful to the writer and to the discussion at large. If you like something about the story, tell us why it works. If a sentence or passage feels off, share what makes you think so. This kind of nuanced analysis is hard work and requires serious critical thinking!


3. Be respectful. It takes guts to share writing. Please be sensitive to the care your classmates have taken in crafting their stories, and to the diverse, often personal subject matter we are likely to encounter.


4. Be ruthless. Being respectful does not mean only saying nice things about people’s stories. Workshop exists to make us better writers, and that doesn’t happen without some serious hatcheting. Balancing respect and ruthlessness means directing your criticism at the piece, and never at the writer.


5. Be thick-skinned. On the receiving end of this ruthlessness will sometimes be your work. Remind yourself that criticism is not personal, that it exists to help you make great work, and that no single piece of feedback—negative or positive—carries the final say.
